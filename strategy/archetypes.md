**SLATED ARCHETYPES**

*Who We Build For, Who We Market To, and Who We Avoid*

Version 3.0 \| February 2026 \| Internal Strategy Document

**1. Purpose of This Document**

This document defines the people Slated is built for. It is the
foundation that informs product decisions, marketing targeting, channel
selection, and messaging. Every other strategy document references these
archetypes.

**What this document does:** Defines behavioral patterns, pain points,
and motivations for our target users. It identifies who converts, who
pays, and who we should avoid spending money on.

**What this document does not do:** It does not define our product
positioning (see Product Positioning), our competitive stance (see
Competitive Positioning), or our brand voice (see Brand Guidelines).
Those documents build on top of the personas defined here.

**2. The Optimization Framework**

Every household that plans meals is optimizing for something. When
resources are finite---mental capacity, energy, money, patience---people
make trade-offs. Understanding which trade-off a person defaults to
tells us whether they will pay for Slated and why.

We organize the market around four primary optimization drivers:

**Why this framework matters operationally:** It is a filter, not just a
taxonomy. When evaluating a marketing channel, keyword, or creative
concept, the first question is: does this attract people who optimize
for cognitive relief or harmony? If it attracts cost-optimizers or
hobbyists, we are burning money.

**The overlap reality:** Real people are blends. A user might be 70%
convenience-driven and 30% harmony-driven. That is fine. The question is
whether convenience or harmony is the primary trigger that gets them to
install and pay. Cost-primary and quality-primary users will not convert
regardless of secondary motivations.

**3. The Hidden Mental Workload**

Before defining the individual personas, we need to define the problem
they share. Both the High-Velocity Parent and the Peacekeeper suffer
from the same underlying burden. The difference is which dimension of
that burden hurts the most.

**3.1 The Full Cognitive Chain**

Dinner is not one task. It is a chain of cognitive work that runs in the
background constantly. The visible part---cooking---is the smallest
piece. The hidden workload is everything that happens before a spatula
is touched:

**1. Recipe Selection**

This is not \"picking a meal.\" It is holding the full matrix of
constraints in your head simultaneously: What does everyone in the
family actually eat? What are the dietary requirements and restrictions?
What did we have recently (so we don't repeat)? What fits the schedule
tonight---is it a 20-minute night or do we have room for something
longer? What ingredients do we already have? What's in season? What's
going to create leftovers we can use? This is not a search problem. It
is a constraint-satisfaction problem that the CHO runs mentally, every
single day, for every single meal.

**2. Preference Management**

The CHO carries a mental database of every family member's preferences,
aversions, allergies, phases, and moods. One kid hates broccoli. The
spouse is doing low-carb this month. The toddler will only eat beige
food. The teenager announced they're vegetarian on Tuesday. None of this
is written down anywhere. It all lives in the CHO's head, and it shifts
constantly.

**3. Shopping List Construction**

Once the recipes are selected, every ingredient needs to be
cross-referenced against what's already in the pantry and fridge. What
do we have? What do we need? What quantities? Are there substitutes for
things we're out of? This is an inventory management task that most
households do from memory, which means it's error-prone and cognitively
expensive.

**4. Shopping Execution**

The list then has to be executed---either through a store trip or a
delivery order. This includes navigating the store or app, making
substitution decisions on the fly when items are out of stock, and
managing the budget. For grocery delivery, it includes translating the
mental list into the specific product SKUs the service carries.

**5. The Background Process**

The most draining aspect is not any single step. It is that this process
never stops. It runs as a background process in the CHO's mind all week.
\"What are we doing for dinner tomorrow?\" is not a question they answer
once---it is a question their brain is always partially working on. The
cognitive load is not measured in minutes spent planning. It is measured
in the mental capacity it consumes while they are trying to do
everything else in their life.

**4. Archetype 1: The High-Velocity Parent**

**Classification:** PRIMARY TARGET

*\"I don't want to build a plan. I want to sign off on one.\"*

**4.1 The Core Behavior**

This person has optimized or outsourced most recurring decisions in
their life. They are willing to pay for solutions that eliminate
cognitive overhead---not because they can't cook, but because dinner is
the last frontier of unmanaged mental load in their household.

They do not need recipe inspiration. They do not need a tool that helps
them plan. They need the entire cognitive chain---recipe selection,
preference management, shopping list, shopping execution---handled for
them. When someone asks \"What's for dinner?\" they want to have an
answer without having spent any brain cycles getting there.

The defining characteristic is not income level or job title. It is the
willingness to trade money for cognitive relief and the expectation that
software should do the work, not present them with options to work
through.

**4.2 The Mental Load (What Hurts)**

**The Cognitive Capacity Problem**

The HVP's life is already running at full cognitive capacity. Work,
kids, logistics, health, relationships---all competing for the same
finite mental bandwidth. Dinner planning is not painful because it takes
a long time (though it can). It is painful because it takes mental
capacity they do not have to spare. The background process of \"What are
we eating?\" competes with everything else their brain is trying to do.

**The Blank Page Problem**

Existing apps present a search bar or a prompt and ask the user to
initiate. That is work. This person needs a finished plan delivered to
them for review---not a blank canvas that requires assembly. The
cognitive cost of starting from scratch every week is what drives them
to takeout, not laziness or inability.

**The Health Guilt**

They order takeout not because they want to, but because it is the only
option requiring zero cognitive investment. The guilt of feeding their
family convenience food is the emotional tax they pay for their
exhaustion. They want to eat well but cannot afford the mental capacity
cost of planning well.

**4.3 The Dinner Draft for the HVP**

For this persona, the Dinner Draft is not about collaboration---it is
about further reducing their cognitive involvement. They do not even
have to be the approver. They push the plan out to the family, the
family reviews and approves (or flags issues), and the HVP is out of the
loop entirely. The plan goes from \"system generates it\" to \"family
ratifies it\" without the HVP being the bottleneck at any stage.

This transforms their role from \"Executive who reviews and approves\"
to \"Executive who delegates and moves on.\" The Dinner Draft is not a
collaboration feature for them. It is a delegation feature.

**4.4 Motivations**

-   Reclaiming cognitive capacity for work, family, and self.

-   Eliminating the background process of dinner planning from their
    mental load.

-   Maintaining health standards without the cognitive cost of achieving
    them manually.

-   Having one less system to manage in an already over-managed life.

**5. Archetype 2: The Peacekeeper**

**Classification:** PRIMARY TARGET

*\"Your family's input before dinner. Not their complaints after.\"*

**5.1 The Core Behavior**

This person carries the family's food system. They suffer the same
hidden mental workload as the HVP---the full cognitive chain of recipe
selection, preference management, list building, and shopping---even if
they may have more time to do it. The cognitive chain is still a burden.
Having more time to be burdened does not make the burden lighter.

But what makes the Peacekeeper distinct is the additional layer of
emotional labor on top of the cognitive load. They are not just
exhausted by the planning. They are hurt by the family dynamics that
surround it. The nightly \"What's for dinner?\" question feels less like
curiosity and more like an accusation that devalues the invisible effort
they put in. When the family complains about the meal---after the
Peacekeeper has spent their cognitive energy planning, sourcing, and
preparing it---the pain is personal.

Their goal is not gourmet perfection. It is harmony. They would
sacrifice \"perfect\" for \"peaceful\" any day. The defining
characteristic is not employment status or income. It is the experience
of carrying a cognitive and emotional load that no one else in the
household sees, shares, or appreciates.

**5.2 The Mental Load (What Hurts)**

**The Hidden Cognitive Workload**

The Peacekeeper runs the same cognitive chain as the HVP: recipe
selection under constraints, preference management across every family
member, shopping list construction, shopping execution. This is real
work even if the Peacekeeper has more available time. The burden is
cognitive, not temporal. Having four hours instead of one to plan dinner
does not reduce the number of constraints you have to hold in your head
simultaneously.

**The Burden of Solo Ownership**

They are the only person holding the plan. No one else in the household
is tracking the preference matrix, managing the pantry inventory, or
thinking about what's for dinner on Thursday. This is invisible work
that goes unnoticed until something goes wrong. The only feedback loop
is negative.

**The \"What's for Dinner?\" Trigger**

This question is not an inquiry. It is a signal that the family is
looking at them to do the work---work they have already done. It
devalues the effort they have put in. This compounds when the family
complains about the choice, or when someone says \"I don't want that\"
after the groceries are already bought. No one wants to help decide.
Everyone wants to complain about the result.

**The Short-Order Cook Trap**

To avoid conflict, they default to making multiple meals or always
deferring to the pickiest eater. This is not a cooking problem. It is a
negotiation failure. They need a system that gets everyone aligned
before the cooking starts, not compliance after.

**The Emotional Dimension**

When the Peacekeeper puts cognitive effort into planning a meal that
meets everyone's needs and then someone complains, the pain is not
logistical. It is emotional. It feels like their effort---their
invisible, unrecognized effort---has been dismissed. Over time, this
creates resentment that is rarely about food and almost always about
feeling unseen.

**5.3 The Dinner Draft for the Peacekeeper**

For this persona, the Dinner Draft is transformative. It solves the core
emotional problem: the Peacekeeper is no longer the sole owner of the
plan.

-   **Before Dinner Draft:** The Peacekeeper plans alone, presents the
    meal, and absorbs the complaints. The effort is invisible and the
    feedback is negative.

-   **After Dinner Draft:** The Peacekeeper pushes the plan to the
    family. The family reviews, votes, and flags issues. The plan is
    ratified by consensus. When someone complains on Wednesday, the
    answer is: \"You already agreed to this.\"

The Dinner Draft does not just reduce workload for the
Peacekeeper---though it does that too by getting the family involved in
the constraint-satisfaction process. More importantly, it makes the
invisible work visible. It creates a record of family input. It shifts
the dynamic from \"Mom/Dad decided and I don't like it\" to \"We all
decided and we committed.\"

This is why the Dinner Draft is the Peacekeeper's activation feature.
The Ready-Made Plan reduces cognitive load (which helps). But the Dinner
Draft addresses the emotional pain, which is what makes this persona
fundamentally different from the HVP.

**5.4 Motivations**

-   Getting family buy-in before the cooking starts.

-   Making invisible effort visible and shared.

-   Reducing the emotional labor of daily negotiations.

-   Having a system the whole family can see and participate in.

-   Reducing the cognitive burden of the planning chain (the same
    motivation as the HVP, just secondary to the emotional one).

**5.7 Strategic Importance**

**This is our blue ocean.** Per the Competitive Positioning analysis, no
competitor addresses the interpersonal friction of dinner. Jow,
Hungryroot, Ollie, and Paprika all treat meal planning as a
single-player activity. The Peacekeeper persona is where Slated has zero
competition.

**The marketing challenge:** The HVP problem is easy to express in a
search ad (\"meal planning done for you\"). The Peacekeeper problem is
harder to compress into a keyword. This persona may be better reached
through content marketing, social proof, community engagement, and
word-of-mouth than through search ads. Channel strategy should reflect
this asymmetry.

**7. Excluded Personas**

These are not bad people or unimportant market segments. They are people
whose primary optimization driver conflicts with what Slated sells.
Marketing spend on these segments will result in poor conversion rates,
high churn, or both. The purpose of this section is identification and
avoidance.

**7.1 The Budget Optimizer**

*\"I will work harder to save \$5.\"*

**Why we exclude them:** Our value proposition is cognitive relief, sold
at \$7.99/month. The budget optimizer values dollars over mental
capacity. They will never pay a subscription to offload work they are
willing to do themselves. Their optimization function directly conflicts
with ours.

**Identification Signals (Negative Targeting)**

-   Search queries: \"cheap meals,\" \"budget groceries,\" \"save money
    on food,\" \"frugal meal planning.\"

-   Coupon/deal app usage: Ibotta, Flipp, Honey.

-   Behavior: price-comparing across multiple stores weekly, buying in
    bulk primarily for savings.

-   Content consumption: budget-focused food blogs, extreme couponing
    communities.

**7.2 The Perfectionist**

*\"I will work harder for better food.\"*

**Why we exclude them:** If the research is their hobby, our automation
is a bug, not a feature. We remove the search-and-discovery process they
actually enjoy. They will feel constrained by curation and want control
over every ingredient and technique. This is the opposite of
\"ready-made.\"

**Identification Signals (Negative Targeting)**

-   Search queries: \"authentic,\" \"from scratch,\" \"best recipe
    for,\" \"heirloom,\" \"traditional.\"

-   Heavy users of recipe management apps: Paprika, NYT Cooking, Yummly.

-   Content consumption: culinary influencers, food photography
    accounts, chef content.

-   Community: r/Cooking, r/AskCulinary, serious foodie forums.

**7.3 Edge Cases**

**The budget-conscious HVP:** A person who also cares about grocery
costs is still a valid target if their primary behavior is
convenience-seeking. The test: would they pay \$7.99/month to not think
about dinner? If yes, the secondary cost-consciousness does not
disqualify them.

**The quality-curious Peacekeeper:** A parent who enjoys cooking on
weekends but needs weeknight relief is a valid target. The test: do they
want Slated to handle the weeknight grind so they can enjoy the weekend
cooking? If yes, the hobbyist tendency does not disqualify them.

**General filter:** The excluded personas are defined by their PRIMARY
optimization driver. If someone's primary driver is cognitive relief or
harmony and their secondary is cost or quality, they can still be a
Slated user. If cost or quality is primary, they will not convert.
